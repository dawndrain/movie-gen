# THE CUTOFF — a Donner Party film (storyboard, first pass, fast mode)

Historical drama, 1846–47. The Donner–Reed wagon train takes Lansford Hastings'
untested "shortcut" to California, loses a month in the Wasatch and the Great
Salt Lake Desert, and is sealed into the Sierra Nevada by the first blizzard.
Of the 87 who took the cutoff, 48 came out.

> Framing device: 13-year-old survivor **Virginia Reed** writing her real May 1847
> letter to her cousin Mary ("never take no cutoffs and hurry along as fast as you
> can"). Her lines are SPARSE assembly-mixed narration per the Variance lesson —
> never sent to Seedance, trimmed to fit windows, bridging cuts. All other dialogue
> is on-camera.

Target: ~31 shots + 4 title cards, ~4.5–5 min, 480p **fast** draft pass.
Tone: restrained, unsentimental; the horror stays off-screen (see CONTENT
SAFETY at bottom — this story is a minefield for the nsfw filter and for taste;
staging discipline is the whole game).

Location plates: `prairie_train` (mile-long wagon column in spring grass),
`salt_desert` (blinding white flats, heat shimmer), `wasatch` (wagons in a
boulder-choked aspen canyon), `truckee_lake` (dark lake, granite wall of the
pass, storm clouds), `cabin_interior` (log walls, ox-hide roof, grease-lamp),
`alder_tent` (brush-and-canvas shelter buried to the ridgepole), `summit_snow`
(white ocean of drifts, snapped pine tops), `johnsons_ranch` (green foothill
valley, adobe, spring blossoms), `napa_porch` (1847 ranch porch, oak trees).

## Eras / wardrobe locks

- **ERA-TRAIL (Apr–Aug 1846):** spring/summer light, dust, healthy faces, clean
  period clothing — slat bonnets, wool trousers, braces, NO modern anything.
  Oxen (not horses) pull the wagons; Virginia on her pony.
- **ERA-DESERT (Sep–Oct):** scorched autumn light, alkali dust on everything,
  clothes ragged at hems, animals gaunt, faces sunburnt.
- **ERA-SNOW (Nov–Mar):** blue-gray light, deep snow, SNOW anchor variants only,
  everyone wrapped in blankets and quilts over their era clothing, breath fog.
- **ERA-AFTER (May 1847):** green California spring, Virginia clean, mended
  dress, healthy again but eyes older.

Every prompt carries the era lock + "1846 overland emigrant clothing, no modern
fabrics or zippers" + the standard negative block ("photorealistic, natural
human motion, no slow motion, no text/captions/subtitles, characters keep
exactly the same faces and clothes as the reference images").

## Voice locks (every dialogue shot)

- REED: firm, clipped, proud American baritone — a man used to being obeyed.
- MARGARET: low, steady, exhausted-warm American alto.
- VIRGINIA: bright young American girl's voice, plain and unactorly.
- GEORGE DONNER: slow, warm, elderly farmer's rumble.
- TAMSEN: precise, quiet schoolteacher's diction, New England flavor.
- STANTON: gentle, mild, unfailingly courteous tenor.
- BREEN: soft Irish brogue, prayerful cadence.
- KESEBERG: German-accented English, controlled, unsettlingly calm.
- EDDY: flat, hard frontier drawl.
- MARY GRAVES: clear young American voice, steadier than the men's.
- Always: "There is no narrator and no voiceover; only characters visible on
  screen speak, lips moving." (Virginia's letter VO exists ONLY at assembly.)

## Music (Sonilo — instrumental only, no vocals)
- Open/prairie cue: warm Americana — fiddle-and-drone, unhurried (under a1–a2).
- "Omen" stinger: low strings + a single far-off church bell; first use at
  c7_first_death, reuse at d4 morning-after and e6 tamsen walking back. One
  motif, three hits — per the Long Game lesson.
- Forlorn Hope bed: sparse, cold — bowed bass, wind-like sustains, no pulse
  (under d2, d6 at 0.08–0.10).
- Relief/paradise cue: strings blooming into major at e8, carries through f1
  and the end card. This is the ONE warm cue — sincerity concentrated late.
- Silence under all cabin dialogue and the Stanton scene. Score the moments,
  not the eras.

## CONTENT SAFETY (nsfw-filter + taste plan — read before prompting)

The Walter's Deal lesson applies with force here: **if two rewordings fail,
change WHAT HAPPENS on screen.** Pre-emptively:

- **Never show or name the taboo.** No prompt anywhere contains cannibal-adjacent
  words (flesh, corpse, remains, butcher, devour, "eat the dead"). The film
  conveys it entirely through d4's morning-after staging (people sitting apart,
  averted eyes) and Virginia's VO — which is assembly-side and never touches
  Seedance. e7 (Keseberg) is a two-shot of faces and an unanswered question:
  no pot contents, no props beyond the buffalo robe.
- **b4_snyder:** the fatal blow happens BEHIND the tangled oxen; prompt only
  "the two men grapple, hidden by the animals; Snyder falls to the ground; Reed
  stands back with empty hands, horrified." If it still bounces, cut from the
  whip hitting Margaret straight to Snyder already on the ground.
- **d6_two_shots:** gunfire is audio-only and distant, shooter never framed.
  If "two distant gunshots echo" trips the filter, replace with: the column
  walking, Eddy stopping to look back at an empty ridge, Foster's place in the
  line empty — and let the VO carry it entirely.
- **d4 Dolan delirium:** "tears off his coat" only — no further undressing
  language (appearance-adjacent wording has drawn nsfw flags before).
- **Starvation makeup:** "hollow-cheeked, exhausted, wind-burned" — avoid
  "emaciated/skeletal" in image prompts if Nano Banana balks (it flagged
  appearance-focused wording on portraits before).
- **Children in peril:** keep children asleep/carried/held in storm shots;
  never a child alone in distress on camera (c2's collapsing line is given to
  a mother, not a child, for this reason).
- **Word check before batch:** "banished", "hang him" (b5) are plausible
  filter-bait — have neutral rewrites ready ("he rides out alone tonight").
