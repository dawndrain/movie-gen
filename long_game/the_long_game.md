# The Long Game

## I. The Arcade

The retro floor was where you went to be seen not caring.

Deshawn was up on the DDR pad doing a song from before any of them were born, moving at maybe a third of the speed the arrows demanded, and he was losing on purpose, and he was making losing look like the coolest thing a body could do. Milo filmed it. Cass didn't watch. Cass — seventeen, and carrying it like a sentence he'd been handed rather than a fact about himself — was reading the maintenance placard on a machine two aisles over, the way you read a thing when you want an excuse to be standing somewhere.

"You're doing the placard thing again," Milo said, not lowering the phone.

"I'm not doing a thing."

"He's doing the placard thing." Deshawn missed another arrow with theatrical grace. "Bro reads the safety warnings on the skee-ball."

They were, all three of them, disgustingly smart. This was 2044 and they'd been optimized in the way everyone their age had been optimized — the tutors folded into the backs of their skulls since they were four, the small clean edits to the code that had made them a little quicker, a little steadier, a little less prone to the diseases that used to thin a generation. They could each have told you the free energy of a reaction or conjugated a dead language. What they could not do was leave each other alone for eleven consecutive seconds, which is the other thing about being seventeen, the part no one optimizes out because no one has figured out that it's a problem.

"The Deep End," Milo read, over Cass's shoulder, in the voice of a man announcing a boxer. He'd stopped filming. He'd found something better. "They put the Deep End back."

The Deep End was at the far wall, past the light-gun cabinets, in the part of the arcade that the arcade itself seemed embarrassed by. It was a single reclined chair inside a matte housing the color of a tooth, with a headset on an articulated arm and a slot for credits and a screen that just said, in a font chosen to be reassuring and failing:

FULL-IMMERSION LIFE SIMULATOR One credit ≈ one subjective year. You will not remember the outside while inside. You will remember everything when you leave.

And below that, taped over the slot on a strip of yellow, the placard Cass had actually been reading, two aisles away, the whole time:

TEMPORARILY OUT OF SERVICE.

There is a specific fear that a broken ride at a fairground produces — not the fear of the ride, which is designed and rated and survivable, but the fear of the broken, the sense that a thing built to be safe has slipped, quietly, out of the category of safe, and no one has told you into which category it slipped. The Deep End, out of service, produced exactly this fear, and the three of them walked toward it the way boys walk toward exactly this fear, which is to say fast, and jostling, and pretending to be dragged.

"It's off," Cass said. "It says it's off."

"It says temporarily," Deshawn said, which is not what it said, and dropped into the chair. "Watch. I'll do a quick one. Easy mode. Five years. I'll be a — " he considered the ceiling — "I'll be a ski guy. Downhill. Gold medals. Chalets."

"Biathlon," Milo said. "Skiing plus guns. More medals."

"Biathlon. Obviously." Deshawn pulled the headset down over his eyes and reached blind for the panel. "Set me up."

Milo set him up. Milo, it should be said, was the one who read the actual documentation — not the placards, the docs, the settings buried three menus deep — and Milo was the one who found that OUT OF SERVICE meant only that the operator-facing safeguards had been pulled for maintenance and never reinstalled, which meant that the difficulty ceiling was uncapped, and the exit-on-demand toggle was live and switchable, and the compression clock was unlocked — the machine could run a hard life faster on the outside than it was ever rated to run an easy one — and that the placard, like all placards, simplified: the memory seal scaled with the difficulty, so that the low settings left a pilot light of you burning inside the life — that was what made easy easy — and the ceiling buried you entire. The whole machine was, in the way of a thing left half-disassembled, more powerful than it was ever supposed to be, not less. Milo filed this away. Milo was seventeen too.

Deshawn's five years took about ninety seconds on the outside. His hands twitched. His mouth moved. Near the end he laughed, out loud, at something none of them could see. Then he tore the headset off, gulping, eyes wet and wide and delighted, back in his body all at once.

"Sochi," he gasped. "Two golds. I — there was a woman, there was a whole — " He looked at his own hands like they belonged to a stranger who'd led an interesting life. "And the whole time I sort of knew? Like a dream where you know it's a dream — it still counts, it's still real, but there's this thread. And I retired at like twenty-six and it got so boring, you guys, retirement is so boring, and there was this old lady who ran the lodge, and one morning she just looks at me and goes, still having fun, dear, or ready for bigger things? And I said bigger things, and I was out." He was babbling, high on it. "Okay. Okay okay okay. Cass. Cass."

"No."

"Cass has to."

"He has to," Milo agreed.

Here is the thing they got right about Cass, the thing that made him theirs: he could be goaded. Not into anything cruel — he'd never once been goaded into anything cruel — but into the chair, into the dare, into the deep end, yes, because underneath the placard-reading there was a boy who wanted, badly, to find out what was down there, and who needed his friends to hand him the permission he wouldn't give himself.

He sat. He pulled the headset down.

"Harder than his," he said. "I'm not doing biathlon."

"Way harder," Milo promised, and meant it, and set the difficulty not one notch above Deshawn's but as high as the uncapped machine would go, which no operator would ever have allowed, and then — grinning, because he was seventeen, because Deshawn was grinning too, because it was funny, because it would be so funny — reached past the safety and flipped the exit-on-demand toggle to OFF.

"Have a good life," Deshawn said.

The credits went in. The slot ate them. The screen, for Cass, went white.

## II. Day Zero

He was cold, and the cold was wrong.

That was the first thing, before he had a self to notice it with: a wrongness of cold, a wet mineral chill coming up through what he'd later understand was packed earth, into a body that expected a chair. Then smell — woodsmoke and animal and something fermented and something rotting, all of it close, none of it filtered — and then, arriving late, like a passenger who'd missed the first stops, a person named Cass who did not, for a while, know he was named Cass.

The sim did what it was built to do. It did not drop a modern teenager into a hut and let him scream. It seated a life. He was a boy of the village, and he had always been a boy of the village, and he had a mother whose face he knew the way you know your mother's face, and he had a name in a language with no written form, and the twenty-first century was not so much forgotten as never, a dream you can't have because you've never slept.

He lived eleven days like that.

They were not good days by his later standards and they were the whole of the world at the time. He carried water. He was cuffed on the ear for spilling water. He learned that the far field was cursed and the near stream was clean and that his uncle's cough was the kind of cough that meant the uncle would be gone by the cold months. On the eleventh day he drank from the near stream, which was clean, which everyone knew was clean, and three days after that the gripe took him — the churning and the flux, the terrible emptying, the thirst that no water answered because the water came right back out of him — and on the fourth day of it, light and dry as a leaf, he died on the packed earth that had been the first cold thing he ever felt.

And woke up. On the packed earth. Cold. Day Zero.

With, this time, all of it.

The thing people never understand about remembering — Cass would think about this for what amounted to centuries — is that it isn't like being told. Being told, you're in a simulation, is a sentence. Remembering is the arcade rushing back into him in full color, Deshawn's wet delighted eyes, the yellow tape, Milo's have a good life, the placard, the placard, the placard — all of it slamming home into a body that was eleven days from dying of something a bottle of clean water and a spoon of salt could have fixed, and Cass sat up on the packed earth and made a sound that his village mother came running for.

"Off," he said, in a language he suddenly had two of. "It's off. It says it's — Milo. Milo. Turn it off, turn it off, I want out, I want — "

His mother held him. She thought he was fevered. In a sense she was an extremely sophisticated process running on a machine in an arcade and she thought he was fevered and she held him exactly the way a mother holds a fevered child, and that would matter later, that she held him exactly right, though it would take him three lifetimes to understand why it mattered.

He tried the obvious thing. If Deshawn could tear off a headset, Cass could tear off a headset; there was no headset. He clawed at his own face and found only his face. He screamed the words that should have been a command — exit, quit, end, out — and the words were only words, went nowhere, meant nothing to the world of earth and smoke.

Then he tried the other thing, the thing he would not later be proud of and would not later be ashamed of either, because it wasn't despair, it was debugging. If dying reset the day — and it had, he'd watched it, he had the empirical result — then maybe dying was the exit. Maybe you got three, like an arcade cabinet, three lives and then the machine spat you back into the chair. It was a hypothesis. He tested it the way you test a hypothesis when you are seventeen and certain and wrong: quickly, and more than once, and each time he opened his eyes on the same cold morning of Day Zero, with his mother not yet awake, the smoke not yet lit, the uncle not yet coughing, the whole world rewound to the same held breath.

There was no counter. There was no lives remaining. There was only Day Zero, patient as a wall.

He stopped testing. He lay on the earth in the dark and did the thing that is the actual beginning of every story like this, the thing the tech tree is built on top of and could not exist without: he accepted the constraint. This is the machine. These are its rules. I don't get to want it to be a different machine. I have to learn the one I'm in.

The rules, as he mapped them over the next several Day Zeros — living a week, dying, restarting, living a week, changing one variable — were these:

The world was deterministic until he touched it. Left alone, the same dog barked at the same moment; the same neighbor dropped the same pot. But the determinism was sensitive — one different word from him on the first morning and the day forked and kept forking, the small change compounding until by evening the village was in a state that his notes (kept in his head, in a memory palace he built and rebuilt) could no longer predict. It was a real simulation with real physics, not a diorama. The people were not scenery. When he let his uncle die eleven times, the uncle died eleven different deaths, said eleven overlapping but never identical last things, and Cass began — only began, here — to suspect something about the people that he was not ready to think all the way through.

And there was the counter he did find, eventually, not by looking but by asking. There was a woman at the edge of the village whom everyone called touched, who talked to the air, and on a whim, on maybe his fortieth Day Zero, Cass went to her and said the true thing, said I am from far away and far ahead and I am trapped in a made world and I want to leave it — and the touched woman looked at him with eyes that were, for one sentence, not touched at all, and said:

"Then you'll have to live it. All of it. To the far end. Forty summers, child, before this place will let you go on."

And went back to talking to the air, and never said a true thing again, no matter how many Day Zeros he spent trying to make her.

Forty. He was, in this life, perhaps seventeen. He had to reach forty. That was the gate. He didn't know yet that it was the first of several gates, or that the number would climb, or that reaching it was the easy part. He knew only that the exit was at the far end of a life, and the only way out of the game was to play the game, and the game was: don't die, for twenty-three more years, in a world that killed you for drinking water.

Fine, thought the boy who read placards. Fine. Let's read the whole machine.

## III. Bronze — The Arrogant Life

He knew so much. That was the problem, though it took him a full life to find out it was a problem.

He was from 2044. He'd had a tutor in his head since he was four. He carried, in the compressed and confident way of the very well-educated young, the entire shape of the tech tree — he knew where it went, knew that germs caused the gripe and that a spoon of salt and a pinch of sweetness in clean water would hold a dying child in the world long enough to recover, knew that bronze was copper with a tenth part tin and that iron was better and harder to get, knew crop rotation and the water wheel and the arch and movable type and the steam engine and the dynamo, knew it all the way you know the route on a map you've never walked.

And here is the thing that shaped his whole first life in the machine, the assumption under everything, the one he wouldn't learn was wrong until it was far too late to matter: he thought forty was the door home. The touched woman had said live it to the far end, forty summers, before this place will let you go on — and Cass heard go home, heard the arcade, heard Deshawn and the headset and the bright clean evening of 2044 waiting on the other side of the gate. Reach forty and wake up in the chair. That was the model. And if reaching forty dumped him back into his own century, then nothing he did here counted, nothing he built here would outlast the four minutes it took on the outside, this whole world was a corridor he had to walk to its end and then leave forever. Why would you carve the sum of human knowledge into stone for the benefit of a simulated future you were about to escape? You wouldn't. You'd do the minimum. You'd survive to the door.

So he did the minimum, and the minimum happened to be godlike, and that was the trap.

And because he thought none of it counted, the thing he chased hardest wasn't glory or good — it was comfort. He was seventeen and filthy and a very long way from home, and what he wanted, with an ache that surprised him, was to be clean.

This is where he learned what a tech tree actually was, and it broke something in his confidence he never fully repaired.

He wanted a hot bath and real soap. Trivial. He was from 2044; he knew how soap worked — fat and lye, and lye you got by leaching water through hardwood ash. Easy. He'd have it by the weekend. Except the ash-lye came out at strengths he couldn't predict, weak one batch and skin-eating the next, and to control it he needed to measure it, and he had nothing to measure with; and the fat had to be rendered clean, which took a vessel that held heat evenly, which the village's crude pottery would not do; and hot water in any quantity meant fuel and a cauldron, and a cauldron meant bronze in a sheet, which meant a smith who could pour flat and large, which the village could not; and the smith, if he'd existed, would have wanted paying, which meant surplus, which meant — Cass stood in the mud one afternoon having back-chained, link by link, from I want a bath all the way down to I would need to reorganize the entire economy of this valley, and he understood for the first time, in his body rather than his head, that the tech tree was not a menu. It was a pyramid, and every block sat on the blocks beneath it, and you could not simply reach up and take the one you wanted; you had to be standing on everything below it first. He got, in the end, a lukewarm sponge-down with a greasy proto-soap that half-worked and smelled of tallow, and it was almost clean, and the almost sat in his chest like a stone. You could not shortcut your way to comfort in a world that hadn't built the floor to stand it on.

He started with the salt water, and it worked, and it was glorious. The flux came through the village in the wet season the way it came every year, and every year it took the small children and the old, and this year Cass moved through it with a clay bowl and a formula, clean water and salt and honey, and children who should have gone light and dry as leaves instead kept their water and kept their lives, and the village looked at the boy who had died of the gripe himself last summer — they remembered no such thing, but he did — and began to look at him the way people look at someone the world listens to.

That was the drug. He'd found the drug in his first dose. The world listens to me.

He gave them the tin ratio and better bronze. He tried, once, to give them the near stream boiled — but he was clumsy with it, he told them the truth, he said there are things too small to see that live in the water and crawl into you, and they heard a boy claiming to see invisible demons, and it did not take, it did not take at all, and three children died that he could have saved, and Cass — who thought none of it counted, who thought these were shadows on a wall he was leaving — shrugged it off in a way that should have frightened him and didn't. (Some part of him filed it anyway. The knowledge was never the hard part. He wouldn't understand what he'd filed for three eras.) He gave them a deeper plow and a better yoke for the ox, and the fields gave more, and more food meant more people who didn't have to farm, and Cass — nineteen, then twenty-two, then twenty-five, rising on a column of grain and clean water and listened-to words — became, without ever quite deciding to, a king.

He taught them games, too, in the idle evenings — a board of holes and pebbles he half-remembered from home, a betting game with knucklebones — because a homesick boy will build a toy before he builds a plow, and the village took to them the way people always take to games, which is faster than they take to anything good for them. He thought nothing of it. He'd think about it a great deal later, in a room with a headset in it.

It is a very good feeling to be a bronze-age king when you have a twenty-first-century head and you think the whole thing is a ride you're about to step off. He spent like a man who knew the money was fake. He took what he wanted. He drew, without caring, the attention that kings draw, and he was clumsy enough and lucky enough to keep his throat uncut — bought off the priesthood he'd rolled over, married a potter's daughter with clever hands because it steadied the succession and because she made him laugh, ruled a valley for fifteen years with the bored competence of a man playing a game he's already sure he's won. He never wrote a word down — never gave them a script, never pressed a tablet, never built a hall — because a library is a gift to the future, and he did not believe, yet, in the future.

The one thing he did leave, he left without noticing it was the only thing that would last. Early on, before the crown, when he was still just the boy the flux hadn't killed twice, he'd wanted the village to boil its water and they'd heard him raving about invisible demons — so he'd stopped telling the truth and started telling a story. He taught them the water spirits were offended by water that had not been greeted, and that greeting the water meant bringing it to a rolling dance of bubbles over the fire and speaking the words over it, and only then was it fit for a child or a birthing mother or the sick. He'd done it to trick them, cynically, a shortcut past an argument he was tired of having. He never thought about it again. He was too busy being king. He had no idea he'd just done the single most durable thing of his entire reign — that a decree dies with the king but a prayer gets taught to children, and a prayer gets carried when a people move, and a prayer does not die when a man dies.

He grew tired of it, in the end, the way Deshawn had gotten bored of his gold medals — been there, ruled that, the throne a chore by thirty-five and a cage by the last of it. He reached forty, which the touched woman had promised was the door, forty summers, and he was a tired old king of forty in a bronze-age body that felt far older, sitting in his hall on the night of the gate, and the touched woman — a mad slave muttering in the corner, she was in this life too, she was in every life, he'd stopped being surprised by it — caught his eye and nodded, once, and was talking to the air again before he could nod back. And Cass braced for the chair, for the headset, for Deshawn's hands and the bright clean arcade evening he'd structured an entire life around walking toward.

The world went white.

And came back. He was lying on a bed — raised off the ground, with a frame — and the light came in through a window with a lattice, and somewhere below him a fountain was running, and it was not the arcade, it was not 2044, it was not the chair, and Cass lay very still while the floor of everything he'd assumed dropped out from under him.

Go on, the touched woman had said. Not go home. Go on. He'd misread the one instruction the machine had ever given him plainly, and he'd built a whole life on the misreading, and now he understood two enormous things at once, and the second one was going to cost him more than any death in the machine had.

The first was that he had kept his age. He was not seventeen again. Whatever this life was, it started where the last one ended, forty years into a lifespan he was beginning to suspect he possessed exactly one of, distributed across time like a river split into channels. He did the bookkeeping right there in the strange bed, because bookkeeping was what he had: 2044 counted. Seventeen years is a life, and it had been his first — the vault every later life would be spent from, the one that held the whole tech tree. The Bronze valley had been his second. This, whatever it was, was his third.

The second was that it had all counted. Every era fed the next. The world he woke into was built on the foundations of the world he'd left — which meant the Bronze life he'd treated as a corridor, a ride, a set of shadows he was leaving behind, had been the ground floor of everything, and he had built almost nothing on it on purpose, had refused to write a single word down, had let the knowledge die with him because he hadn't believed in a future that turned out to be the very ground he was now standing on. He had been given the foundation of the human world to pour, and he had poured the minimum, and now he had to live on top of the minimum.

How completely he'd wasted it came clear while he was walking the streets of the town he'd woken into — a real town, brick and tile and a grid of streets, a forum, an aqueduct walking on its arches across the valley on legs of stone. He passed a public fountain where a woman was filling a jar, and before she filled it she paused, and murmured something over the water, and made a small circling gesture, and Cass stopped in the street as though he'd walked into the arch of the aqueduct itself.

He knew the gesture. He'd invented the gesture. It was the greeting of the water, worn down by — he did the arithmetic and had to sit on the lip of the fountain — by more than a thousand years, carried across a distance he couldn't imagine on the backs of grandmothers, and the woman filling her jar had no idea it had ever meant boil, had no idea a strange quiet man in the hills had made it up to trick her ancestors into not dying, thought it was simply what you did, simply piety, simply the shape of a wise thing. She didn't even boil the water anymore; the meaning had rubbed off and only the gesture was left. It saved no one now. And it was his, the last living trace of an entire lifetime, a hand moving in a circle over a jar.

This is the moment Cass stopped being a tourist in the machine and started grieving in it. Because the seed had survived and the seed was almost nothing, and he understood, sitting on the fountain's lip in a town his own long-dead village had, in some attenuated way, helped make possible, that everything he built would come to this — a gesture, a word, a remedy no one could source — and that this was not nothing, and was so much less than he'd wanted, and was, in fact, the actual mechanism by which the whole world had been built: numberless people giving numberless small durable seeds to the dark, most of them lost, a few of them surviving as the reflex of a hand over a jar.

He went looking, that first year, for the rest of himself. If a gesture could cross a thousand years, what about the true things — the metals, the medicine, the knowledge? He haunted the town's libraries and the temple archives and the collections of the rich; he learned the age's letters in a month and read everything a bronze-age king might have echoed down into. And he found nothing of the knowledge — but he found, in a collection of translated hill-country legends a rich man kept for their quaintness, himself. A king, it said, of the deep past, in a valley to the north, who had raised the dead with clean water and claimed to see demons living in it, and who had gone to the gods in a single night at the very height of his glory, and whose name — the scribe noted it as a curiosity — the people still used, faintly, as a word for a clever fool. There he was. His whole Bronze life, forty summers of grain and clean water and listened-to words, cooked down to a fable and a joke-word for a show-off. He read his own obituary in a stranger's parlor and it was four lines and one of them was an insult, and he laughed, because the alternative was the other thing, and he filed that too — the specific vertigo of finding the record of a self you outlived, waiting in an archive for you to walk in centuries late and read how it ended.

Of the knowledge itself there was nothing, because he had left nothing. He'd invented nothing to outlast him, written nothing down, trained no one who understood — he'd spent a whole reign certain none of it counted, and the machine had taken him at his word. What he knew had gone into the ground with a king the hill-country remembered only as a cautionary joke. Only the ritual lived. Only the thing he'd hidden inside how people already prayed — the one gift he'd given carelessly, to win an argument, without ever meaning it to last. The knowledge he'd been proud of died with him unrecorded. The trick he was faintly ashamed of crossed a thousand years. He'd had it exactly backwards for an entire lifetime, and he only learned which was which by walking into a Roman street and watching a woman greet a jar.

He sat with that for a long time, and it rearranged him.

He had a lot more life to live. And this time he would not treat the world as a corridor — he knew, now, that there were no corridors, that every floor was the foundation of the next, that he was building the very ground his later self would have to stand on. He got up off the fountain and went to see what a thousand years had made, and what he could add to it that would last — now that he knew what adding to it actually meant, and how brutally hard lasting actually was.

## IV. Roman

The Classical world was, to Cass, a workshop with the tools finally in it.

The Bronze Age had been survival with his hands tied — no clear glass, no strong acids, no way to get a fire hot enough or a measurement fine enough to do anything but the crudest chemistry. Here there was infrastructure. There were roads and a coinage and mines and a class of people who didn't have to farm and could therefore be paid to make things. Here, for the first time, he could actually build, and here is where he let himself go deep, because he had a rich base and a plan.

He did not become a king. He'd learned. He became a workshop owner, a foreign engineer with strange methods and reliable results, useful enough to be wealthy and boring enough to live.

And he found her in the first month, this time — he'd learned that too. A beggar woman in a doorway off the forum, talking to the air, and when he stopped in front of her she looked up with eyes that were, for one sentence, not touched at all. "Fifty summers, this time," she said, and went back to the air, and no coin he ever gave her bought a second sentence. Fifty. Ten years. He went and bought the workshop that afternoon.

He built water first, because water is free power and needs no fuel. He knew the secret the age was fumbling toward: the overshot wheel, water dropping onto the top of the wheel and turning it by weight rather than shoving it from below by current, near three times the power from the same stream. He built a mill that ground grain so cheaply that the town's bread got cheaper and Cass got quietly rich, and then he did the thing that mattered more than the wheel, which was to build a second wheel that made things other than flour. He geared it to bellows, and the bellows to a furnace, and now he had a fire he could make hotter than the age could make by lung and arm — and heat, Cass knew, is the gate that all of chemistry and metallurgy waits behind.

With real heat he made better steel — not the whole Bessemer future, he couldn't reach that, but crucible steel, iron and the right measure of charcoal sealed in a clay pot and held at white heat until the metal drank the carbon evenly and came out hard and uniform instead of the lottery the age's smiths were used to. He didn't explain carbon. He gave them a procedure, exact and ritualized, this weight of this black stuff and this many hours at the color of the noon sun, and the procedure worked whether or not anyone understood it, which was the entire point, which was the Bronze Age lesson applied: make it a practice, not a theory.

And then, because he had heat and glass — the age had glass, cloudy and precious, but Cass knew the fluxes to clear it and the patience to blow it thin — he reached for the thing he'd been building toward, the thing that is the quiet god of all industry: acid.

He made vitriol. He roasted green vitriol — the pretty green crystals the age already knew, iron sulfate, weathered out of certain ores — in a clay retort with a glass beak he'd blown himself, and drove off a heavy vapor, and caught it, cooled, as oil of vitriol: sulfuric acid, the single most useful substance a chemist can have, the thing that dissolves and purifies and pickles and etches and stands at the head of a thousand reactions like a doorman. It was weak and it was filthy and it was real, the first strong acid in the world made on purpose by a man who knew what it was for, and with even a little of it Cass could do a hundred small magics the age called miracles and he called Tuesday.

He tried, this time, to give it away — he'd learned the Bronze lesson about knowledge dying with a man, and he wanted this one to live. It went nowhere, and the way it went nowhere taught him more than any success. He explained the process, freely, to anyone who'd listen: it was smelly, it was dangerous, it ate the clothes off you, and it produced a substance whose usefulness he could demonstrate but not, in terms the age had, explain. The dyers had their own way and didn't want his. The smiths had their own way and didn't want his. His first apprentice learned half of it and left to marry into a respectable pottery. His second copied the recipe down wrong, produced something that took his eyebrows off, and told everyone the foreigner's art was cursed. Cass, who had imagined himself handing the world a gift, watched people back away from it with the polite firmness of a man declining a second helping. He got exactly one taker in ten years, a grave young apothecary who liked that it cleaned wounds, and that — one person, who thought of it only as wound-wash and never knew it was a doorman to a thousand rooms — was the entire yield of the most important chemical in the world, offered free to a city. The knowledge was never the hard part. Getting anyone to want it was the hard part, and people did not want the useful thing; they wanted the thing that fit the life they already had.

But the apothecary wrote things down. He was, in the only sense that mattered, the one man in the city who did — proportions, in his own hand, in a working book he kept — and so Cass, half in gratitude and half in defeat, gave him the rest of what would fit in a book like that. The salt-and-honey water for the flux, dictated in measures: this much salt, this much honey, a jug of clean water, and keep giving it however fast it runs through them. The washing of hands before wounds and childbeds. Small kitchen things, things that needed no furnace and no theory, and the grave young man copied them down under wound-wash without a flicker, one more foreigner's receipt in a tradesman's book, and Cass walked home thinking of it as the consolation prize — the crumbs of a feast the city had declined. He was wrong about that in exactly the way he'd been wrong in the Bronze Age, in exactly the same direction, and he wouldn't find out for three hundred years.

They wanted, for instance, ice cream, though none of them knew it yet, and neither did he until he tried to make himself some.

This is the era where the back-chain finally closed, and it closed on something gloriously stupid, and it was the best thing that happened to him in a thousand years. He wanted, on a killing-hot Mediterranean afternoon, something cold and sweet. In the Bronze Age that want had bottomed out in reorganize the economy; here, he realized, standing in the market doing the arithmetic, the chain might actually terminate in things he could buy. Cream: the market had it. Honey and fruit for sweetness: the market had it. Cold — cold was the problem, cold in high summer, and here Cass found that the Roman world had already built him most of the ladder without knowing what it was for. The rich packed cellars with winter snow and river-ice, insulated under straw, and sold it through the hot months; he could buy cold. But cellar-ice only gets you to just-melting, and just-melting won't freeze cream, and this is where the twenty-first-century head earned its keep, because Cass knew the one counterintuitive thing the age did not: that if you pack ice with enough salt, it gets colder as it melts, drops well below freezing, pulls heat out of anything you nest inside it. Salt on ice makes it colder. Nobody in the world knew that but him — and even he knew it as a fact without a number, because the tutors handed you facts and the numbers had stayed home in 2044. So he got the number the way he got everything now: a row of jars on a bench, snow weighed into each, salt weighed in against it in rising measures, and in every jar a thimble of cream to be the judge, because there was no thermometer in the world and the cream was the thermometer. Too little salt and the cream stayed cream. Past a certain point, more salt bought nothing but a bigger salt-bill. The cold bottomed out near one measure of salt to three of snow — colder than any winter he'd ever stood in — and would not go lower for love or money, and he wrote the ratio down. Then he set a bronze pot of sweetened cream inside a larger vessel, packed the gap with the ice-seller's snow and a fortune in salt at the proven ratio, and turned it, and scraped it, and turned it, and swore at it — the churning was its own small discovery, that you had to keep it moving or it froze to a brick instead of a cream — and produced, on a bench in the ancient world, sweating and triumphant, a dish of something no human being had ever tasted.

He ate the first bowl in the bath.

It was the high-water mark of five lifetimes, and it deserves its whole picture: the Roman world had baths, real ones, hot rooms and hot water and better soap than his Bronze self had bled for, and none of that was his, all of it simply there for a workshop-owner with money, waiting for him to stop speedrunning long enough to sit in it. So he sat in it. He lowered himself into water hot enough to sting, in a tiled room going soft with steam, with a bowl of impossible cold sweetness in his hands that was his, that he'd dragged link by link out of the tech tree by main force — the fire and under his skin the cold, the summer outside and the frost in the bowl — and Cass, who had been a bronze-age king and a boy dead of dirty water and a man who'd back-chained from a bath all the way to despair, sat in the hot and ate the cold and thought, with his whole chest, I have made it. Whatever this is, wherever this is — I have made it. And the only thing wrong with the moment, the small true thing that would matter more than all the rest, was that the person he wanted to hand the second bowl to was a glassblower named Faustus, and wanting to share it was the first crack in the wall between Cass and the idea that the people in here were real.

He was making a life of it, and it was a good life, and it is the life in which he did the thing he was most ashamed of, and learned the thing that changed him most.

He'd never stopped probing the machine. Old habit, from the cold Day Zero mornings — but the probing had changed. In the Bronze Age it had been frantic, a trapped animal testing the bars. Here, older, wealthier, no longer in a hurry, it became something quieter and stranger: he wanted to know if the people were real. Not real like scenery, which he'd settled long ago — real like him, real all the way down, with insides that persisted when he wasn't looking. He couldn't rewind them to check, so he did what a patient man does instead. He watched. He asked the same question of the same person on different days and listened for whether the answer stayed consistent the way a real memory stays consistent. He told people secrets and came back a year later to see if the secret had cost them anything to keep. He told himself it was a question about the simulation and not about them, which was the lie inside the experiment, because you cannot study a person that closely without coming to love them, and he did.

The man he loved was a glassblower named Faustus, quick and warm and funny, the best friend Cass had had in any life. And what undid the experiment was not a result but the friendship itself — the slow accumulation of a real thing, Faustus confiding one night about a dead son in a way no diorama would ever bother to simulate, grieving it fresh and particular and years old all at once, and Cass understanding in his chest what he'd been trying to prove with his head: that the man was as real as grief gets, that they all were, that he was not a player in a world of shadows but one more mortal in a world of mortals who happened to know a secret about the walls. He stopped probing that day. He'd needed to be sure they were real. The cost of being sure was becoming a person who could never again treat a real thing as a variable — and he never did, in any life after, and was gentler for it in a way he couldn't have explained and never tried to. He kept Faustus's confided grief like a stone in his pocket for the rest of that life and never told him he knew.

And he did, this era, the thing the Bronze Age had shamed him into: he tried to leave a legacy that would last. He'd learned that lesson in a temple archive, reading nothing where his whole first life should have been. So this time he wrote. God, he wrote — years of it, in the evenings, in the age's own good letters that plenty of people could read: the overshot wheel and the crucible procedure and the vitriol and the fluxes for clear glass and the beginnings of everything above them, the tech tree itself, drawn and described and cross-referenced, scroll after scroll of papyrus, the sum of a twenty-first-century head poured out in a language a thousand hands could copy. He housed it in the town's library, donated in his own workshop's name, and he thought, the way you think a thing right before the machine teaches you otherwise — this is the answer. This one will hold. This one anyone can read. He never found out, in that life, whether he was right. He died still believing it.

He reached fifty with the vitriol thriving and the overshot wheels turning up and down the valley and Faustus grown old beside him and a whole library of his own knowledge shelved and safe, or so he thought, and on the last night the beggar woman — older now, still in her doorway — met his eye and nodded, and Cass nodded back, on purpose, in time, and the world went white.

## V. Renaissance — The Press, and the Boiler

He woke at fifty into a world of ink.

It was recognizably nearer. Not his — never his, the machine wouldn't simulate his own century until he'd earned the whole climb — but nearer, a walled city of tall narrow houses and a river full of barges and, in the air, the sense of a world about to tip forward, a world that had recovered the workshop of the Classical age and added to it a restlessness the Romans never had. Cass, fifty years old and three lifetimes deep, walked into it and felt the accumulation for the first time as a thing he could see rather than infer.

Because it was ahead. Subtly, wrongly, promisingly ahead of where the history in his old tutor's lessons said it should be. The waterwheels were overshot and everywhere. The steel was too good, too even, for the year — someone, somewhere upriver, had learned to pour it consistent and never learned they were early. And he found, in a physician's shop, a remedy for the flux printed on a card, old wisdom, source unknown: a jug of clean water, this much salt, this much honey, and keep giving it however fast it runs through them. His measures. His words, worn smooth by three centuries of copying, but his — and not the king's version, which had died unrecorded with the king. The apothecary's version. The consolation prize, the crumbs he'd handed one grave young man because the man kept a book, and the book had been copied, and the copies had been copied, and the feast he'd offered a whole city had come to nothing while the afterthought crossed three hundred years. He had to stand in the street again. The channels of his river were rejoining. Nothing survived with his name on it — no tablet, no wheel, no workshop — but the water was clearer and the steel was harder and the sick children of this city died a little less than they should have, and it was him, diffused past recognition into the baseline of a world, which is the only form of immortality the machine, or possibly anything, actually offered.

He found her in the first week — a printer's widow who talked to the air in the yard behind her dead husband's shop — and she said, "Sixty, this time, child," and never a true word after, and Cass bought the shop, partly for the presses and partly, though he never said it, to keep her near.

But of the library — of the years of scrolls he'd died believing would hold — there was nothing. He went looking, the way he always went looking now, and pieced it together from a chronicle: the town he'd worked in, the collection he'd donated, gone. A fire, generations after him, a lamp and a dry summer and a wall of shelved papyrus and old wood, the dull regular weather that happened to libraries in the ancient world. A decade of his evenings had gone up in a column of smoke he never saw, and taken the tech tree with it, and left not one legible line. And this — arriving too late to even watch it burn, learning of the loss the way you learn a stranger died — was where the lesson finally set in his teeth, the one underneath the Bronze lesson: it was never only that he'd failed to write things down. It was that the medium itself was the bottleneck. One copy of everything, however legible, is one fire away from nothing. Papyrus rots and burns; a scribe copies one scroll in a season and makes his own errors doing it; knowledge held in a few fragile places, recopied by hand, loses ground to entropy faster than it gains. You could not out-write fire and forgetting one scroll at a time. You needed a medium that copied itself faster than the world could destroy it — that flooded the world with so many copies no fire could ever find them all.

He needed the press. And this city, ahead of its history because of him, was already on the very edge of it.

And this time — this was the decision that shaped the whole life, and it was the opposite of every decision before it — he decided to hold nothing back. In the Bronze Age he'd given the minimum because he thought nothing counted. Here he understood exactly the opposite: that everything counted, that this era's gift became the next era's foundation, that a decade spent flooding this world with knowledge would surface, when he woke again, as a world centuries further along. He was done being careful. He was done losing to fire and forgetting. He was going to print the tech tree itself — all of it he could reach, as fast and as cheap and as widely as the machine would let him — and let the compounding do what compounding does.

The press was the key that turned everything else. There was block printing in the city; there were scribes; there was rag paper. What there wasn't, yet, was movable type, and the deep problem of movable type is not the idea — the idea is obvious — but the tolerances, the fact that you need thousands of little metal letters all exactly the same height so the page prints even, and to get them the same you need a way to cast to a standard, an adjustable mould and a punch and a matrix, precision manufacturing of a thousand identical small things, which is a problem the age had never had to solve because it had never before needed a thousand identical small things. So Cass's Renaissance life was, at its technical heart, a story about making the tool that makes the tool. He didn't invent printing. He invented the hand mould — the clever adjustable box, the punch struck into soft copper to make a matrix, the type-metal — and the type-metal is where the tutor-fed head finally showed him its holes. He knew the ingredients: lead, with tin to make it flow, and antimony for the one property that mattered — antimony expands slightly as it cools, so the cast fills the mould instead of shrinking from it. What he did not know was the proportions, because nobody memorizes proportions when you can look them up, and there was nothing left anywhere to look anything up in. So he found them the way he'd found everything since Day Zero: fair trials, one change at a time, every cast numbered, every result written down. Too little antimony and the letters came out soft and slurred under the press; too much and they went brittle and snapped in the setting-stick; a whole winter of test bars before the counts closed on a window — near four parts antimony and one of tin to fifteen of lead — where the metal took a crisp face off the punch and held it through a thousand pressings. It cost him a season to rederive what one page of his old tutor had held, and it was the cheapest tuition of any of his lives: knowing the answer, it turned out, was not the knowledge. The recipe was the knowledge. With the mould and the metal he could cast a hundred identical letters an hour, and with the letters he could set a page, and with the page he could print a thousand copies of a thing in the time a scribe copied one.

And then he poured his whole head onto paper and sold it for the price of the paper. Everything he could reach went into cheap plain pamphlets and cheaper broadsheets. The germ theory of disease, drawn so a barber-surgeon could follow it — wash the hands, boil the instruments, keep the childbed clean — no spirits, no demons, just these are the animals too small to see and here is how you kill them. The overshot wheel and the crank and the camshaft. Crop rotation and the nitrogen-fixing beans. The clear-glass fluxes, and lenses ground from clear glass, and what you saw when you put two lenses in a tube and pointed them at a fingernail or a star. Oil of vitriol and the acids and salts you could make downstream of it. Anatomy drawn true, the blood going round in a circle. Double-entry bookkeeping, so a merchant could tell profit from ruin. The mechanical clock, the logarithm, the decimal point. He wrote them plain, wrote them cheap, wrote them so a clever apprentice anywhere could build from the page, and he made the press itself easy to copy on purpose, so that within a few years there were a hundred presses along the river copying his pamphlets and printing their own, and the knowledge got away from him — which was the entire point. You cannot burn a thing that exists in ten thousand copies in ten thousand hands. He had finally built the medium that beat fire, and he filled it with everything he had, and he could feel the century tilting under the weight of it while he was still alive to see.

He printed one pamphlet, too, that was not a technology — a plain short thing about how to find out whether a thing is true: try it both ways, change one thing at a time, count the result, write the count down, let others check it. It was the antimony winter boiled down to a single page. He didn't oversell it. But it was cheap, and it got used to wrap fish, and the men who wrapped fish read the wrapping, and a few of them tried it, and it had a way of turning a person who read it into a person who generated more of the pamphlets Cass hadn't lived to write.

He nearly died making steam.

That's the part he'd tell you not to skip, if he told you anything. He got greedy, near the end, at fifty-eight — the press was loose and self-sustaining and he had a year or two spare and he wanted, badly, to give the climbing world power, to skip it forward. He knew the steam engine. He knew it cold. He built a boiler — a great riveted iron drum to raise steam and drive a piston and pump a mine dry — and he knew the theory so well that he got careless about the thing theory doesn't teach, which is that a boiler is a bomb you are choosing to stand next to, that the iron of the age was uneven and the rivets were hand-set and the pressure inside a sealed drum climbs faster than intuition says, and he had no gauge because no one had yet made a gauge — and here is the part he'd skip if the telling let him: he knew how to make one. He'd sketched it, a long glass tube standing up off the steam space with mercury in the bend, the pressure legible in inches of climbing silver, and he'd set the sketch aside because blowing and calibrating that much glass would cost him a season, and he was fifty-eight and impatient. So he was reading the strain by ear and by the color of the fire like a fool with the instrument unbuilt in his pocket, and one grey morning the drum let go.

It killed two of his workmen and it killed Cass — and the machine did to him exactly what it had always done, and this time the cost was ruinous. He woke at fifty, at the era's beginning, in the world of ink with nothing built — the press ungiven, the pamphlets unprinted, ten years of the most careful work of any of his lives simply gone, because he had died, and dying cost the era, and he had known that and gotten greedy anyway. There was no undoing it, no shortcut back; the machine had one price for death and it collected in full. He had to build all of it again from the start — the whole press, the whole slow fight to give the knowledge away — just to arrive back at the yard, older inside if not out, standing in front of the same boiler, having paid years to learn one thing. And the thing he'd learned, the thing that cost him a decade twice over, was the antimony lesson again, taught this time in iron and two men's lives: knowing the answer is not the same as being able to build it, and the gap between them is made of exactly the boring things — tolerances, materials, the gauge, the safety valve — that the excited amateur skips. This time he spent the season. He blew and calibrated the mercury gauge first and mounted it where he could read it from anywhere in the yard, and then he built the safety valve — a weighted lever on a port, that lifts and vents when the pressure passes what the iron can bear — the whole difference between an engine and a bomb, one honest instrument and one simple valve, built by the fear of a man who'd already been killed by their absence. This time the boiler sighed instead of screaming, and the piston walked up and down, and the mine pumped dry, and Cass was too shaken to feel anything he'd have called pride.

He reached sixty tired and quieter than he'd ever been. He'd given the world the press and then poured a century of knowledge through it, and one working engine with a valve on it, and he could feel it all getting away from him into ten thousand hands, uncontainable now, unburnable, compounding — building the rest without him, faster than any world he'd walked before — and the printer's widow met his eye across the print-shop floor and nodded, and Cass, sixty years old and bone-tired and closer to home than he'd been in what felt like a thousand years, nodded back, and let the world go white.

## VI. The Last Era

He woke at sixty into 1926, or a thing wearing its face.

Electric light. He knew it before he opened his eyes, the particular steadiness of it, no flame-flicker, and he lay in a narrow bed in a boarding-house room with a filament bulb burning above him and, out the window, a city that hummed. Trams. A telephone wire's low singing. Somewhere a wireless set, a voice reading news through static. It was the twentieth century by its face — and, in the way he'd stopped being surprised by and never stopped being moved by, it was wrong, ahead, and this time the wrongness was no half-step. The calendar said 1926; the trams and the collars and the newsprint said 1926; but the capability underneath said something decades further on — the wireless too clear, the grid too complete, the chemist's shelf stocked with medicines whose names he knew from a century that hadn't happened yet. The method he'd wrapped fish in had been loose in the world for four hundred years instead of two, compounding the whole time, and the year had kept the fashions of 1926 while quietly outgrowing the reach of it, and that gap — between the face of the world and the reach of the world — was his fingerprint. All of his fingerprints at once. He'd stopped being able to find any single one of them and he could see all of them in the level of the thing, the way you can't see the individual bricks of a wall you're standing too close to but you can see that it's tall.

He was sixty. He knew, without needing to be told this time, that the last gate was eighty. He found her anyway, in the first week, out of something that had stopped being strategy lifetimes ago and become something more like paying respects — an old woman muttering at the tram stop, talking to the air — and she looked at him and said nothing at all, only nodded, and he nodded back. He didn't need telling anymore, and they both knew it.

Twenty years. And for the first time in every life he'd lived, Cass looked at the number and did not think how do I speedrun it. He thought: twenty years. That's a long time. That's enough to have a life.

So he had one.

He didn't build much, this era, and that was the point, that was the whole shape of the ending arriving. The world was climbing fine without him; the tech he could have given it, it was three decades from finding on its own, and he'd learned across four eras that shoving it forward got your art called cursed, or blew up in a boiler, or burned in a library you never got to watch burn. His job was done. His job had been done, in a sense, since a woman murmured over a jar in a Roman street. What was left was not to build the world but to live in one, the thing he'd never once let himself do, and he was old enough and tired enough and wise enough, finally, to do it.

He took a job that a man with a strange deep competence could take without drawing eyes: he taught. A schoolmaster, in a school in the climbing city, teaching boys and girls the method — not as his invention, God no, just as how you find things out, the fair trial, the one changed thing, the honest count — teaching it as the ordinary furniture of a good mind, which by now, in this world he'd bent, it nearly was. He was good at it. He was, it turned out, born to it, or made into it across sixty years of learning that the count is the master and not the man. His students loved him and forgot him at the ordinary rate that students forget teachers, which is to say incompletely, in a gesture, in a habit of mind, in the reflex of a hand over a jar.

And he married.

Her name was Iris and she ran the boarding house and she was fifty-two and widowed and entirely uninterested in Cass's strange competence and entirely interested in whether he wiped his feet, and Cass, who had been a bronze-age king and a Roman engineer and the man who loosed the scientific method on the world, fell in love with a woman because of the exact and merciless way she made him wipe his feet. He didn't tell her what he was. He'd never told anyone; the one time the truth left his mouth, in a cold village a thousand thousand days ago, it had gotten him called mad. But he came the closest he ever came, one night, old and warm beside her, when he said I feel like I've lived a long time, and Iris said everyone our age feels that, and Cass said no, I mean — a long time, and Iris looked at him in the electric light and said, not distant at all, the way the touched woman had looked at him for one true sentence a thousand years and one machine ago, "I know you do, love. Come to bed." And that was as close as the truth ever got to being said out loud in a life, and it was enough, it was so much more than enough.

They had eleven years. She died at sixty-three of a thing that even Cass's head couldn't fix, and Cass was seventy-one and he grieved her the way you grieve the one real thing, plainly, for a long time, played completely straight, no machine in it at all.

It was in the grieving that he went to the library.

He didn't plan it. He was a widower with too much quiet and he found himself in the reading room of the city library, in the newspaper archive, turning the great bound volumes, and he found her obituary because he wanted to see her name in print, wanted the world's own record to agree that Iris had existed — and there it was, four plain lines, beloved wife of, and the of was him, his name, the name he'd worn in this life, in the newspaper's own type, in the record the world kept of itself. Iris had lived. The paper said so. And beside her, when he turned forward through the years he hadn't lived yet with the strange doubled knowledge of a man who both would and wouldn't, he found — because he made himself look — the place where his own would go. He was seventy-one. He knew the gate was eighty. He knew, with the cold clean part of him that had never stopped debugging, that he had nine years and then an exit, and that the exit meant this — the reading room, the bound volumes, Iris's four lines, the whole world of it — would close like a game, because it was a game, he'd known it was a game since a cold morning on the packed earth.

And he sat in the reading room with his hand flat on his dead wife's obituary and did not want to leave.

That was the thing the machine had been building toward for four eras and he only understood it there, with the newsprint under his hand: that it had taken him a bronze-age lifetime and a Roman decade and a Renaissance and eleven years with Iris to be made into a person who did not want to leave, who valued a life — a small durable ordinary mortal life, a life that was nothing but a long accumulation of greeted water and fair trials and wiped feet and one woman's four lines in a paper — enough that its ending could hurt. He'd come in a seventeen-year-old who tried to die to escape. He was a seventy-one-year-old who'd have given a great deal to stay. The game's win condition wasn't reaching eighty. The win condition was becoming the kind of thing that didn't want the clock to run out, and he'd met it, sitting in a library with his hand on a name.

## VII. The Machine Documented

He found the way out in the last era, and it found him the way everything true had found him — sideways, while he was looking at something else.

His grandnephew by marriage — Iris's family, which had become, in eleven years and nine more of widowerhood, simply his family — was a bright anxious young man named Peter who worked for a firm that made entertainments, and one Sunday Peter came to dinner full of a thing his firm was building, a marvel, a booth you sat in that let you live a whole life in an hour, the papers were going to lose their minds, they hadn't even settled what to call it yet — and Cass set down his fork very carefully and did not let his face do anything.

Because of course. Of course this world, this bent-ahead world that his own long meddling had accelerated, would reach the Deep End early — and would reach it, he understood with a cold that no bath could have touched, partly because of him. He'd carried games into every era he'd lived. The pebble-board and the knucklebones in the Bronze village; dice and a betting game he'd taught a Roman workshop on slow afternoons; a deck of painted cards he'd cut in the print-shop because he was homesick for a screen and a card was the closest he could get. Small things. A homesick man builds a toy before he builds a plow, he'd thought, and thought nothing more of it. But games are a thread like any other, and a world you've taught to love play a little sooner, a little harder, is a world that runs a little faster down the road that ends in a booth you sit in to live a whole life in an hour. He hadn't built the Deep End. He'd have cut off his hand before he built the Deep End. But he'd spent five lifetimes teaching the world to want exactly the thing the Deep End was, and the world, accelerated, had followed the wanting all the way here. It was the water-greeting run backward into dread: his fingerprints on the machine that had swallowed him, smudged so far into the century he couldn't have lifted a single one, and all of them there. His meddling was the reason there was anything to exit from.

He did not play Peter's machine. He'd understood, since a Roman lifetime spent learning that a glassblower's grief was as real as his own, that he would never again go deeper into a thing that made real people to spend. But he went to see it, as a curious old schoolmaster might, and Peter, proud, brought his strange competent uncle into the workroom.

There was a chalkboard by the door with names on it, because the firm was still fighting over what to call the thing — the Century Chair, the Second Life, a dozen others struck through — and while Cass stood there a young engineer, wanting something with more dare in it, called across the room: what about the Deep End? And the room laughed, and liked it, and someone wrote it at the top of the board, and Cass kept his face still for the second time in a month and understood that he would never know — not then, not ever — whether the machine he lived in, asked by its own story to imagine a machine, could only reach for its own blueprints, or whether he had bent this world so true to its source that it converged, all on its own, on the very name. Both readings fit. Both would keep him up.

And then Peter showed him the works — and this is the thing Cass had waited four lifetimes and a whole grieving life for without knowing he was waiting: the documentation.

His own machine, the one he lived inside, had never shown him a single line of its rules. He'd reverse-engineered every law of it from the outside, by dying and counting, the way you'd map a country you were forbidden to see by noting only where the walls were. But this machine — the same machine, the same architecture, invented inside the sim by a world that had to write its manuals — had a manual. Peter's engineers had a maintenance panel, and a diagnostics mode, and a service manual three inches thick, and in it, in plain type, were the rules Cass had spent five lifetimes bleeding to infer: the age gates, the save-point logic, the exit conditions. It was like a man who'd learned a language from ransom notes finally being handed a grammar. He read it in an afternoon while Peter thought he was admiring the craftsmanship.

And on a page near the back, under a heading about testing and overrides, there was a code.

Not a cheat, exactly — an exit. The engineers had needed it for themselves: the only way to test a machine like this was from inside it, living the test lives, and no playtester alive will sit out forty subjective years to file a bug report. So they had built a tester's door — a sequence, a specific thing you did with your attention and your hands, designed on purpose to be carried into the life and executed from within it, by the player, with no panel and no plug. An operator could haul a stuck player out from the outside; a tester could walk out on his own; and the engineers of this bent century had, sensibly, documented both. Cass read the sequence four times until it was carved into the memory palace he'd been building since a cold Day Zero, and he closed the manual, and he thanked Peter for a fascinating afternoon, and he went home to the boarding house that was only his now, and he sat under the steady electric light with the exit finally, fully, in his hand.

He was seventy-one. He could leave that night.

He didn't.

He'd looked at Iris's four lines in the library and he'd become, somewhere across five eras, a person who finished things, who trusted the count and the clock over his own impatience, who understood that a life was worth living to its far end even when — especially when — you held the exit in your hand the whole time. He had the code and he put it in his pocket like a stone, like Faustus's grief, and he went back to teaching the method to children who'd forget him at the ordinary rate, and he grew the last of the way old.

He reached eighty in the spring, in the boarding house, in a body of eighty years that had held a river's worth of lifetimes. He could feel the gate the way you feel a door in a dark room by the change in the air. And on the morning of it, the old woman from the tram stop — muttering, touched, talking to the air, in every era, in every life, the machine's one honest employee — caught his eye through the window as she passed, and nodded. And Cass, eighty years old and ready, more ready than he'd ever been to be seventeen and screaming on the earth, nodded back.

But he didn't wait for the white. He'd earned the gate and he didn't need it. He took the exit code out of his pocket — the tester's door, the documented sequence — and he used the thing five lifetimes had given him, which was the standing to choose his own way out, and he left the last era not because the clock made him but because he was finished, and whole, and done.

The world went white on his own terms, which is the only kind of exit that was ever worth reaching eighty to earn.

## VIII. The Arcade, Again

The headset came off in Milo's hands.

"—so long," Deshawn was saying, mid-sentence, because on the outside it had been about four minutes. "Bro, you were in there forever, the machine was making this — Cass? Cass. Cass."

They were thumping him. Milo had the headset. Deshawn had both hands on his shoulders, shaking him, delighted, and there was a small crowd because the machine had been flashing a warning the whole time, MAXIMUM DIFFICULTY, EXIT DISABLED, and someone had gone for a manager, and the manager was hurrying over with a lanyard swinging, and Cass sat in the tooth-colored chair in the year 2044 and looked at his friends — seventeen, both of them, seventeen and immortal and optimized and immature, who had just, laughing, sent him to live and die across the whole climb of the human world — and felt an old, old man's love for them, and an old, old man's distance, both at once.

"How was it," Milo was saying, grinning, not yet knowing to be afraid. "What'd you do, what'd you do, you were maxed out, nobody plays maxed—"

"I lived a long time," Cass said.

They laughed. Of course they laughed; it was a funny thing to say, four minutes in a chair. They pulled him up and thumped his back and the manager was saying something about liability and Deshawn was already telling the crowd his own biathlon story, and Cass walked out of the arcade into the clean bright optimized evening of his own century in a kind of fugue, his friends orbiting him, and everything was too fast, too clean, too easy, water that no one greeted and no one needed to, and he could not stop feeling the shape of a code in the pocket of a mind five lifetimes deep.

Here is the thing he couldn't put down, walking home under the smart streetlights:

His machine had let him out at eighty. But the manual — Peter's manual, three inches thick, read in an afternoon — had described a machine that could simulate past the player's own present, if you let it, if the gates kept climbing. His had stopped at 1926-that-wasn't because that was as far as it needed to take him to teach him the lesson. But the architecture didn't have a ceiling at the present. The architecture didn't think the present was special. The architecture would happily simulate a 2044, a bright clean optimized 2044 with genetically-tuned boys in an arcade, if that's where a long enough climb happened to land.

Cass walked around his own reality for a day in a daze, and his friends thought he was just rattled, shaken up, you were maxed out bro, take it easy, and on the second night, alone in his room under a light that didn't flicker, in a century that felt exactly one half-step too advanced in a way he couldn't have named before he'd spent five lifetimes learning to feel that exact wrongness in the level of a world — Cass took the code out of the pocket of his mind, the tester's door, the documented sequence, and he did the thing with his attention and his hands.

And the world went white.

And he woke to Deshawn thumping his back.

"—an extra level," Deshawn was crowing, to a different crowd, in a different arcade that was exactly the same arcade. "This genius — this absolute genius — got himself stuck a whole extra level down, on easy mode, who does that, who gets stuck on easy—"

They weren't congratulating him this time. They were ribbing him, howling, because on this level's outside he'd apparently done something spectacularly stupid: they'd put him in on easy, a four-minute lark, and somewhere inside the easy life he'd found the arcade that the game contained, and sat down in the chair that the game contained, and let the friends that the game contained crank the inner machine to a ceiling no operator would ever have allowed — played a whole hard climb of lifetimes one level deeper than the life he was supposed to be coasting through. The machine out here had flagged it on its screen for everyone to read: NESTED SESSION DETECTED. Milo had the headset. Milo was laughing so hard he had to sit down.

"I lived a long time," Cass tried, dazed, and this time they howled, because it was even funnier the second time, a man explaining that his four minutes had felt like a life, when he'd been so dumb he'd nested himself an extra level deep in a thing anyone else exits by just sitting still.

He walked around this reality for a day. Same clean streets. Same steady light. Same friends, same love, same distance. And the wrongness was there too — of course it was there too, he could feel it in the level of the world, one half-step too advanced — but the wrongness was also there in the world he'd thought was base, wasn't it, he'd felt it there too, and now he couldn't tell if he was feeling a real seam or just feeling the shape of his own suspicion, printed on every world he looked at, the way a man who's learned one language in ransom notes hears threats in ordinary letters. Or — and this was the thought he could not finish — the wrongness was the pilot light. Easy mode leaves a thread of you awake; Milo's docs had said so, in an arcade he could no longer swear was real; and if the half-step-too-advanced feeling was that thread, was the part of a buried player that half-knows, then feeling it here meant — and he stopped himself, because that road had no far end, and he had spent five lifetimes learning to live only in worlds that did.

He caught himself, walking, checking the doorways. Looking for her — a muttering woman, touched, talking to the air, the machine's one honest employee, who had stood at the edge of every world it had ever run for him. He didn't find her. And he understood, even while his eyes went on doing it, that her absence proved nothing either way — a machine that could build a thousand years could leave out one madwoman, and a real world full of real people has real women who talk to the air — and he checked every doorway anyway, the whole way home.

On the second night, alone, under the steady light, he took out the code again.

And did the thing with his attention and his hands.

And nothing happened.

The light stayed steady. The room stayed the room. He did it again, more carefully, the exact sequence carved into a memory palace five lifetimes deep — and nothing, the code was just a gesture now, a hand moving in a circle over a jar, meaning nothing here, meaning boil to no one, worn down to reflex in a world that had no water to greet. Either this was the base reality and the code had never been anything but a thing that worked one level down, a door that only opened outward from the room it belonged to — or this was a level so deep the code couldn't reach the surface from here, and he'd never know which, and the not-knowing was the same either way, was a man in a room with a useless gesture and a suspicion he'd carry to the far end.

He sat with it a long time.

Then, slowly, the way a fever breaks, the way water clears when you stop stirring it, the suspicion began to thin. Not because he answered it — he never answered it — but because he was, whatever else he was, five lifetimes into knowing that you live the world you're in, that you accept the machine you're actually inside of rather than the one you'd rather be inside of, that a life is a long accumulation of small durable ordinary things and is worth living to its far end whether or not you can prove the floor is real. The reading room. Iris's four lines. The count is the master and not the man.

He put the code away. Not thrown out — he'd never be able to throw it out — but away, in a pocket, a stone he'd stop reaching for.

There was a knock, and Deshawn's voice through the door, ordinary and immortal and seventeen: "Yo, we're going out, you coming or you gonna sit in the dark being weird about the arcade thing all night?"

And Cass — old past any counting, and seventeen, a bronze king and a Roman engineer and a schoolmaster who'd loved a woman named Iris to the far end of her life, a man who'd loosed a press on a world and diffused past all recognition into the level of its air — Cass stood up, in a room he would never be certain of, under a light that didn't flicker, and found that he wanted to go, that he was, in fact, glad they'd waited for him.

"Yeah," he said. "Yeah. I'm coming."
